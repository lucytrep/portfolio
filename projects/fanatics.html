Overview

Helping fans find the exact item they mean

Sports merch catalogs are huge, seasonal, and filled with near-duplicates (players, alternates, years, editions). Fans often know what they want, but not the exact product name or filters needed to find it.

This concept explores an AI-assisted discovery experience for Fanatics that turns natural language intent into precise browse paths, reduces mis-buys, and increases confidence at checkout.

Catalog complexity + near-duplicate inventory
North star: “Find my gear” → refined results → confident purchase

The Problem

Search and filters don’t match how fans think

Fans search with messy real-world language (“retro Knicks hoodie from last year”, “women’s jersey like the one in the playoffs”), but commerce systems require exact names, season tags, and structured filters.

The result: high bounce, endless scrolling, and uncertainty about authenticity, fit, and edition.

Failure modes: vague search → irrelevant results
Confidence gaps: size/fit, authenticity, edition

Research Insights

Three moments where people abandon

Ambiguous results People can’t tell which items match the moment they have in mind (player, year, edition, game).
Filter overload Too many filters, unclear terminology, and hidden constraints make refinement feel like work.
Purchase anxiety Fit, returns, and “is this the official one?” uncertainty slows or stops checkout.
Journey map: intent → search → refine → decide
Top questions at decision time (fit, authenticity, edition)

Defining the Opportunity

Translate intent into a confident decision

The experience reframes “search” as a guided translation layer: fans express intent naturally, and the system returns a clear result set plus the shortest path to refine.

Design principles • Start with intent: natural language and visual cues • Reduce refinement steps: show only the next most-informative filters • Earn trust: authenticity, edition, and return clarity surfaced early • Keep it fast: minimal typing and quick compare behaviors

Opportunity map: discovery → decision support
Information architecture: Find, Results, Compare, Checkout

Design Direction

Guided search + smart refinement + confident compare

“Find my gear” entry A lightweight prompt that accepts intent (“game”, “player”, “year”, “budget”) and optionally an image reference.
Refinement chips The system proposes the top filters that will meaningfully narrow the set, based on the current ambiguity.
Decision support Side-by-side compare with edition, material, fit guidance, and return policy visibility.
Wireframes: guided entry + refinement chips
Compare view: edition + fit + authenticity signals

Final Product Experience

A faster route to “yes, that’s the one”

The final concept combines guided entry, adaptive refinement chips, and a compare-first shopping flow that surfaces the most confidence-building details early.

Instead of teaching users to search like a database, the system translates how fans talk about teams, moments, and players into concrete product matches.

High-fidelity mocks: results + compare
Checkout confidence: size/fit + returns + authenticity

Impact

Reducing uncertainty in discovery and checkout

Intended outcomes: higher search success rate, fewer refinements to purchase, lower return rates driven by edition/fit confusion, and improved conversion for high-intent shoppers.

Success metrics (concept): search success + conversion
Qualitative outcomes: trust + speed + confidence

Reflection

The product is the translation layer

For large catalogs, the biggest design opportunity is often not adding inventory or more filters—it’s making intent legible, and shaping the fastest path to a confident decision.

What I’d test next: chip suggestions + compare behavior
Future: live drops, team moments, and personalized collections
